Introduction

Vimba Helpline is a free cell phone-based service to help women and children living in Diepsloot, a township in northern Johannesburg, who are victims of rape and other forms of violence get access to help. It is a project of the Mail & Guardian’s Bhekisisa Centre for Health Journalism in partnership with five non-governmental organisations that provide services and support to victims of gender-based violence in Diepsloot: Sonke Gender Justice, Green Door Place of Safety, Lawyers Against Abuse, Afrika Tikkun and the South African Depression and Anxiety Group.

What does the helpline do?

All you need is a cellphone with a charged battery. You don’t need data or even airtime because the service is free of charge. People who dial the number are asked a series of questions about where they are and the type of help they need. When they answer all of them they are sent an SMS telling them where to go to get help in the township and a phone number they can contact. If they indicate that they have been raped, they are also sent an SMS with some information about what to. The helpline also collects data about gender-based violence in Diepsloot.

Data collected by the Vimba app

The Vimba Helpline has two functions. The first is to make is easier for victims of violence and abuse in Diepsloot to access the services and support available to them. The second is to collect information that could be useful to the service providers about the prevalence of violence, areas in Diepsloot that could perhaps be hotspots, and times when violence peaks.

The numbers in the box on the top left are the total number of calls made to the helpline in the selected month as well as the number of calls that were finished to the point where an SMS was sent.

The other charts show only the completed calls.

The chart showing the days of the week on which people contacted the helpline gives an idea of whether more calls are made over weekends than on week days.

The chart showing the type of help selected gives an idea of wheher the people using the helpline are themselves victims of violence or whether they want to help someone else who is a victim. It also distinguishes between rape and other types of violence or abuse.

The pie chart shows the time of day calls were made to the helpline. A day is divided in quarters – midnight to 6am, 6am to noon, noon to 6pm and 6pm to midnight – to make it easier to see whether more calls are made at night than during the day.

The bar chart showing the number of calls made on each day of the month can be used to determine whether more violence occurs at the end of the month when people are likely to have been paid, or during public holidays.

The final bar chart shows the number of calls being made from each of Diepsloot’s 13 extensions. This could provide information about hotspots in Diepsloot where violence is more prevalent and perhaps indicate the need for services in those areas.

About Diepsloot

Diepsloot is a densely populated triangle of land wedged between William Nicol Drive and the N14 freeway in northern Johannesburg. It was established in 1995, and has grown rapidly over the course of two decades as people flocked to Johannesburg in search of work. In 2011 the national census put the population at 180 000, but it’s generally believed that by 2016 at least 350,000 people lived there. It’s a ramshackle dust-coated mixture of tin shacks and RDP houses, intersected by narrow roads, very few of which are tarred.

Unemployment rates are high. A social audit conducted by Sonke Gender Justice found that up to half the working-age population is jobless. The provision of basic services such as water and electricity is limited.

There are very few social and recreational facilities, for example, Sonke’s social audit found that there are only two community halls and one youth centre, which has a library. There is one park in Diepsloot, which was last renovated in 2008, and there is no public swimming pool. There are, however, more than 200 shebeens and taverns.

Gender-based violence in Diepsloot

In October 2013 the bodies of two little girls, one three years old, the other, her two-year-old cousin, were found in a broken public toilet near their home in Diepsloot. Bhekisisa visited the family in 2015 and reported on the disturbing prevalence of rape in the community.

In early 2016 research conducted by the University of the Witwatersrand and Sonke Gender Justice in which 2,600 men, aged 18 to 40 years, living in Diepsloot were interviewed found that rape and physical abuse rates are more than double those reported in national studies.

  • 56% of the men in the study used physical or sexual violence against women.
    • 20% only physical violence.
    • 14% only sexual violence.
    • 29.7% both types of violence.
  • In South Africa 14% of men reported enacting violence towards women.

Part of the Sonke Change Trial is holding workshops with men in Diepsloot with the aim of changing their attitutudes towards women and violence, and in this way reducing the levels of rape and violence against women and children.

Diepsloot has a network of NGOs and community organisations that can offer support to victims of violence. The aim of Vimba is to make sure people know who they can turn to when they need help.

Contact Vimba

Vimba is a project of the Mail & Guardian’s Bhekisisa Centre for Health Journalism. It was made possible by funding from the Bill and Melinda Gates Foundation.

If you would like to find out more about Vimba email info@vimbadiepsloot.org.za

Sonke Gender Justice works across Africa to strengthen government, civil socity and citizen capacity to promote gender equality, prevent domestic and sexual violence, and reduce the spread and impact of HIV and Aids.

Green Door is a temporary place of safety in Diepsloot, where victims of gender-based violence also receive counselling and help accessing services.

Lawyers Against Abuse provides free legal support and counselling to victims of rape and gender-based violence.

Afrika Tikkun provides social workers and support for children. It works with Childline, which helps abused children.

SSouth African Depression and Anxiety Group provides psychological support for victims of violence and rape.

Bhekisisa is the centre for health journalism at the Mail & Guardian newspaper. Bhekisisa has published stories about child rape and gender violence in Diepsloot since 2015. It launched the Vimba helpline as a way to help the Diepsloot community get access to the organisations that can assist victims of violence.
